The Legal Argument

This protest is filed under Texas Tax Code §41.43(b)(3), the unequal-appraisal ground. Under this section, the appraisal district must prove that your appraised value is at or below the median of a reasonable number of appropriately-adjusted comparable properties. The five comps below share your HCAD neighborhood code and grade, fall within ±15% of your living area, and within 10 years of your year built. Because comps vary in size, the comparison is made on a per-square-foot basis — the same normalization HCAD's own mass-appraisal (CAMA) model uses internally, and the basis of the Texas Comptroller's uniformity audit of every appraisal district.

Methodology Note

Alternate Ground — §23.23 Homestead Cap

Verify before filing. §23.23(b) excludes the value of new improvements (additions, new construction, major remodels) from the cap. If the year-over-year jump reflects a real improvement you added, this ground does not apply. Check the parcel's building permits and the HCAD improvement records on hcad.org before claiming the cap at the hearing.

What This Tool Doesn't Adjust For

The per-sqft median test above treats every comp as equivalent to your home after matching on neighborhood code, HCAD grade, living area (±15%), and year built (±10). It does not adjust for the following, and any of them can change whether your appraisal is really unequal:

  • Lot size. HCAD values land separately from improvements. An oversize lot adds appraised value that the per-sqft comparison cannot normalize.
  • Pools, covered patios, detached garages, workshops. These appear in HCAD's total appraised value but live in separate building records; the tool's $/sqft denominator uses only your main dwelling.
  • Accessory dwellings (garage apartments, mother-in-law suites, casitas). Their value is included in your total appraisal but their square footage is not in the per-sqft denominator, so the ratio is inflated compared to neighbors without one.
  • Condition and age-of-systems. HCAD has a CDU rating (condition / desirability / utility) but it isn't in the public download. A home with a 25-year-old roof, failing HVAC, or foundation issues is objectively worth less than a comp in move-in condition — argue this at the hearing with photos and estimates.
  • Recent renovations. Fresh kitchens, additions, and major remodels add value HCAD may not have captured yet; the reverse is true too (deferred maintenance is invisible to the district).
  • Flood history. Post-Harvey and post-Imelda damage, even when fully repaired, legitimately depresses market value. Not in HCAD's data.

Before filing, open your 5 comps on search.hcad.org (the account numbers above are click-to-copy) and compare lot size, features, and condition against your own. If a comp has significant extras yours doesn't, that's a legitimate reason for HCAD's appraiser to defend the gap — know it before walking into the hearing, not during.

How to Use This Report

File by May 15, 2026, or within 30 days of your Notice of Appraised Value — whichever is later. Missing this deadline waives your right to appeal for the 2026 tax year.

Step 1 — File the Protest

Log in to owners.hcad.org using your iFile number (it's on the Notice of Appraised Value HCAD mailed you). When choosing your grounds for protest, select “Value is unequal compared with other properties.” You can attach this report as supporting evidence, but don't rely on HCAD reading it in detail — they won't.

Step 2 — Understand the iSettle Offer

After filing, HCAD will likely respond with an iSettle offer — a small reduction, often in the $2,000–$10,000 range. This offer is not a ceiling. If the reduction is smaller than the gap shown on Page 1, reject it and request a formal Appraisal Review Board (ARB) hearing, where you'll present this report in person (or by video). Most of the value in filing comes at the ARB, not from the iSettle offer.

Step 3 — Your Hearing Script

If the Appraiser Pushes Back

The district's appraiser may try one of these pushbacks. Have the scripted response ready:

The appraiser may say…Your response
“Those comps aren't appropriate.” They share my neighborhood code, grade, sqft band, and age band — the same filters HCAD uses internally. They meet the reasonable-number-of-appropriately-adjusted test.
“Your home has features those don't.” §41.43(b)(3) requires appropriately-adjusted comps, not identical ones. HCAD's own appraisals of these homes already reflect the adjustments the district made for their features.
“Market value supports our number.” §41.43(b)(3) is an unequal-appraisal claim, not a market-value claim. The median of comparable appraisals is the standard this panel is asked to apply.

What NOT to Argue

  • Market value or recent sale prices. Different statute, different argument. Save for §42.26(a)(3) judicial review.
  • “My taxes went up too much.” Irrelevant — the ARB panel sets the appraised value, not the tax rate.
  • Repair costs or damage. That is a condition argument under §41.44, not an unequal-appraisal argument under §41.43(b)(3).
  • What your neighbor paid when they bought. Panels distrust anecdotes; stick to the comp table.

If You Want to Escalate — §42.26(a)(3)

If the ARB denies your protest, you have 60 days to file for judicial review in district court under Texas Tax Code §42.26(a)(3). That statute prohibits the district from presenting — and the court from considering — market-value evidence as a response to your median-of-comps showing. It is a stronger procedural posture than the ARB, but requires a lawyer and filing fee. Mention it at the ARB hearing to signal that you understand the full legal ladder.
